Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is anesthesia?

A: Anesthesia is a medical practice that uses drugs or other interventions to prevent patients from feeling pain or other sensations during medical procedures.

Q: Who administers anesthesia?

A: Anesthesia is typically administered by an anesthesiologist, a medical doctor with specialized training in administering anesthesia and managing patients during and after medical procedures.

Q: What are the different types of anesthesia?

A: There are three main types of anesthesia: general anesthesia, regional anesthesia, and local anesthesia. General anesthesia is used to induce unconsciousness, while regional anesthesia is used to numb specific areas of the body. Local anesthesia is used to numb a small area of the body, such as a tooth or a small surgical site.

Q: What are the risks of anesthesia?

A: Risks include allergic reactions, breathing problems, and blood pressure changes.

Q: What should I expect during and after anesthesia?

A: You will be unconscious or sedated and won't remember the procedure. After, you may feel groggy or nauseous, and your anesthesiologist will monitor you closely.

Q: How is anesthesia administered?

A: Anesthesia can be administered through various methods, including intravenous (IV) injection, inhalation, or nerve blocks.

Q: Can anesthesia cause long-term effects?

A: While most people recover from the effects of anesthesia without any long-term problems, some patients may experience cognitive impairment or memory loss. However, these effects are typically temporary and resolve within a few days to weeks.
